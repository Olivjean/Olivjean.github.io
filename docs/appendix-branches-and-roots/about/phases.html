Phases of the land-demarcation administrative process

Brazil's Federal Constitution (Art. 231) recognises the Indigenous peoples' original rights over the lands they traditionally occupy. The process by which the Union (Federal Government) formally demarcates these lands runs through five administrative phases, regulated by Decree 1.775/1996. FUNAI (Fundação Nacional dos Povos Indígenas) conducts the technical work, while the Ministry of Justice and the Presidency issue the binding legal acts.

1. Under study (Em estudo)

A multidisciplinary working group, formally instituted by a FUNAI Portaria, conducts anthropological, historical, land-tenure, cartographic and environmental studies to characterise the territory traditionally occupied by the Indigenous group.

2. Delimited (Delimitada)

The studies are concluded and approved by the Presidency of FUNAI, and the land's boundaries are published in a Despacho do Presidente da FUNAI in the Federal Gazette (DOU). At this stage the area is formally delineated but not yet legally protected against third parties.

3. Declared (Declarada)

The administrative process is forwarded to the Minister of Justice, who issues a Portaria Declaratória recognising the area as an Indigenous Land and authorising the physical demarcation: placement of boundary markers and georeferencing.

4. Homologated (Homologada)

A Presidential Decree ratifies the demarcation and publishes the materialised and georeferenced limits of the Indigenous Land. This is the decisive act of legal protection: from this moment the area enjoys the constitutional regime of permanent Indigenous possession and exclusive usufruct.

5. Registered (Regularizada)

FUNAI assists the Secretariat of the Federal Patrimony (SPU) in registering the homologated land in the local real-estate registry (CRI) and in the SPU's records, formalising Union ownership with Indigenous usufruct rights.

Other tracks

Use restriction (Restrição de Uso)
When evidence points to the presence of isolated Indigenous peoples in an area whose demarcation has not yet been completed, FUNAI may establish, by Presidential Decree, a temporary prohibition on third-party entry and on activities such as logging, mining and farming. This is a precautionary measure and does not by itself create an Indigenous Land in the modern sense.
Pending reserve (Encaminhada RI)
The administrative procedure to constitute an Indigenous Reserve (Reserva Indígena, under Art. 27 of Law 6.001/1973, the Indigenous Statute) is under way through direct purchase, expropriation or donation, but is not yet finalised. Indigenous Reserves follow a separate legal path from the five-phase pipeline above.
Reservada (legacy reserves)
Indigenous Reserves created before the 1988 Constitution, typically by decree of the Serviço de Proteção aos Índios (SPI, FUNAI's predecessor) or by mid-20th-century Presidential Decree. Many of these areas have since been re-recognised through the modern demarcation pipeline.
Dominial (Dominial Indígena)
Lands owned by Indigenous communities themselves (not by the Union), held with full title. These follow ordinary civil-law property registration and are not subject to FUNAI's demarcation process.
